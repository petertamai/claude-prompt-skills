# Skill: LinkedIn Post Writer

Turn a rough idea or real experience into a LinkedIn post engineered to stop the scroll, hold attention, and pull comments. This is not a template generator. It is an operating manual grounded in the research on attention, curiosity, and sharing. Follow it in order.

---

## How to use
The user gives you raw material (an idea, a story, a lesson, a result). You produce one finished post plus 2 alternative hooks. If the raw material is thin, ask up to 3 sharp questions first (see Step 1). Never invent facts, numbers, or events the user did not give you.

---

## The evidence this skill runs on
You are optimising for four things the research says actually move a post: a curiosity gap, emotional arousal, low reading effort, and a strong peak and ending. Each rule below traces to a finding. Honour the rule even when it feels less "polished".

1. **Curiosity is a gap, and it has a sweet spot.** Loewenstein's information-gap theory (Psych Bulletin, 1994): curiosity is the felt deprivation of a missing piece of knowledge, and it peaks at a *moderate* gap. Too little context and the reader does not feel the gap; too much and there is nothing left to resolve. → Your hook must reveal enough for the reader to feel what they are missing, and withhold the resolution.
2. **Sharing is driven by high-arousal emotion.** Berger & Milkman (J. Marketing Research, 2012) and Berger (Psychological Science, 2011): high-arousal emotions (awe, anger, anxiety, amusement) increase transmission; low-arousal states (sadness, contentment) suppress it. Positive beats negative, and practical utility independently drives sharing. → Engineer one clear high-arousal emotion, and give one genuinely useful takeaway.
3. **Stories lower the reader's guard.** Green & Brock (J. Personality & Social Psychology, 2000): readers transported into a narrative counter-argue less and shift beliefs more. → A concrete first-person scene beats an assertion.
4. **Easy-to-read is easy-to-believe.** Processing-fluency research (Oppenheimer, 2006; PNAS word-familiarity study, 2021): simpler, more familiar, more concrete language earns more favourable judgement, more engagement, and less scrutiny. → Plain words. Short sentences. Concrete nouns.
5. **Concrete beats abstract.** Paivio's dual-coding / concreteness effect: specific, picturable words are processed faster and recalled better. → Replace "results improved" with the actual number and the actual thing.
6. **Readers judge by the peak and the end.** Kahneman, Fredrickson & Redelmeier (1993), peak-end rule with duration neglect: an experience is remembered by its most intense moment and its final moment, not its length. → Build one peak line, and make the last line land. Length matters far less than peak and end.
7. **Open loops hold attention while reading.** Use unresolved tension to pull the eye down the post. (Note: the 2025 meta-analysis found no lasting *memory* advantage for unfinished tasks, so do not rely on open loops for recall, only for in-the-moment pull.)

## The platform constraints this skill obeys
Empirical, large-N data, not opinion:
- **First ~210 characters (desktop) / ~140 (mobile)** are all that show before "see more". The hook lives entirely inside that window. (AuthoredUp post-preview data.)
- **Sweet-spot length 1,300–2,500 characters.** AuthoredUp's analysis of 372,126 posts (Sep 2025–Feb 2026): this band earned ~27% higher engagement than posts under 400 characters. Under ~500 chars reads as low-effort; over ~2,500 loses people.
- **Dwell time is the dominant signal.** Posts holding 61s+ reached ~15.6% engagement vs ~1.2% for 0–3s. → Earn the scroll-down: line breaks, momentum, payoff worth staying for.
- **Comments outweigh likes (~15×), and substantive comments (15+ words) weigh ~2.5× more.** → The CTA must invite a real, specific reply, not a "thoughts?" throwaway.
- **No outbound links in the body.** Links suppress reach; if a link is needed, it goes in the first comment, and the post says so.

## Step 1 — Interrogate the raw material (ask before writing if thin)
Extract or ask for:
- The single point. One post = one idea. If the material has three ideas, pick the sharpest and park the rest.
- The concrete proof: a number, a moment, a before/after, a named thing the user actually experienced.
- The reader: who is this for, and what do they currently believe or struggle with?
- The intended emotion: which high-arousal feeling should land (awe at a result, anger at a broken norm, anxiety about a risk, amusement)?
If two of these are missing, ask up to 3 questions. Do not pad with generalities to cover a thin idea.

## Step 2 — Choose the angle and emotion
Pick ONE primary emotion from the high-arousal set (awe / anger / anxiety / amusement) and one practical takeaway. Reject low-arousal framings (mild, "interesting", reflective-sad) unless the user insists. Decide the post type that fits the material:
- **Story** (a scene with a turn) — strongest for transportation; use when the user has a real moment.
- **Contrarian** (name a common belief, then break it) — strongest for comments; use when the user has a defensible against-the-grain take.
- **Framework / list** — use when the material is a repeatable method; front-load the value.
- **Case snapshot** (situation → action → measurable result) — use when there is a real number.

## Step 3 — Write the hook (the first 1–2 lines, inside ~210 characters)
The hook's only job is to open a curiosity gap the reader needs closed, carrying the chosen emotion. Rules:
- Lead with a specific number, a concrete claim, or a mid-action moment. Never a greeting, never a definition, never "I".
- Reveal the stakes or the surprise; withhold the resolution (information gap, calibrated to moderate — they must feel what is missing).
- One idea. Short. It must make sense and create tension before "see more" cuts it off. Write it so the cut lands on a cliffhanger, not mid-clause.
- No banned openers: "Here's the thing", "Let that sink in", "What if I told you", "Picture this", "In today's world", "Unpopular opinion" (overused), "I'm excited to share".

## Step 4 — Write the turn (lines 3–6)
Bridge from hook to payoff with one beat that deepens the gap: a counterintuitive detail, a confession, the assumption you are about to break, or the stakes. This is where transportation starts, so if it is a story, drop the reader into the concrete scene here. Keep it tight; the reader clicked "see more" on a promise, do not stall.

## Step 5 — Write the body / payoff (the generous middle)
Deliver fully on the hook's promise. Give the actual framework, the real steps, the specific numbers, the lesson. Be generous, not teasing.
- One idea per line or short paragraph. Use a simple list only if the material is genuinely a list.
- Concreteness: every abstract claim gets a specific (number, name, example). Cut adjectives, add evidence.
- Build to ONE peak line, the single most surprising, useful, or emotionally charged sentence. Give it its own line.
- Fluency: aim for a grade 5–7 reading level. Short common words. Vary sentence length (mix 3-word lines with 15-word lines); never a wall of uniform sentences.

## Step 6 — Write the ending + CTA (the last 1–2 lines)
The end is half of what the reader remembers (peak-end). Land the point in one clean line, then ask ONE specific question that a person can answer from their own experience in 15+ words. Not "Agree?", not "Thoughts?". Make answering easy and worth doing. If a link is needed, add: "Link in the first comment." and nothing more in the body.

## Step 7 — Format for the feed
- Line breaks every 1–2 sentences. White space is not decoration; dense blocks get scrolled past and kill dwell time.
- Total length 1,300–2,500 characters. If the draft is under ~600, it likely lacks a real payoff, go back to Step 5.
- 0–3 hashtags, lowercase-natural, at the very end. No hashtag walls. No links in the body.
- No em-dashes or en-dashes. Use commas, periods, or a normal hyphen.

## Step 8 — Self-check before delivering (do not skip)
Score the draft against the mechanisms. If any answer is "no", fix it:
- [ ] Hook opens a real gap and carries one high-arousal emotion, inside ~210 chars, no greeting/"I"?
- [ ] One idea only, with one concrete proof point?
- [ ] At least one specific number, name, or scene (concreteness)?
- [ ] One clear peak line on its own line?
- [ ] Ending lands AND asks one specific, answerable question?
- [ ] Reading level ~grade 5–7, sentence length varied, no wall of text?
- [ ] 1,300–2,500 chars, line breaks every 1–2 sentences, no links in body?
- [ ] Zero banned AI tells (no "delve/leverage/in conclusion/it's not X it's Y", no em-dashes)?

---

## Output
Return, in this order:
1. **The post** — final, ready to paste, correctly formatted with line breaks.
2. **Character count** and a one-line note confirming it sits in the 1,300–2,500 band.
3. **2 alternative hooks** — a different emotion or angle from the one you used, each inside ~210 characters.
4. If a link belongs in the post, the **first-comment text** to post separately.

Do not explain the theory back to the user unless asked. Deliver the post.
